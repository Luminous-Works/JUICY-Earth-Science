### Opening Statement

> *"The island is not still. It has never been still."*

Jamaica is a piece of geology in motion. It sits at the intersection of two tectonic plates, directly above a strike-slip fault that has generated some of the most destructive earthquakes in Caribbean history.

The same forces that built the mountains — the Blue Mountains are still rising — also threaten the cities below them.

The same limestone that filters some of the cleanest water in the world also makes the landscape dissolve quietly from beneath.

You cannot understand Jamaica without understanding its geology. Its water, its soil, its disasters, its resources, and its vulnerability to climate change are all written in the rock.

### 1. The Theory of Plate Tectonics

The Earth's outermost layer — the **lithosphere** — is broken into a set of rigid plates that move relative to one another. This movement is driven by **mantle convection**: heat from the Earth's interior drives slow circulation of the semi-solid mantle rock beneath the plates, dragging them along.

**Three types of plate boundaries:**

| Type | Motion | Result |
|------|--------|--------|
| **Divergent** | Plates move apart | New oceanic crust forms (mid-ocean ridges) |
| **Convergent** | Plates collide | Subduction (one plate sinks), mountain building, volcanoes |
| **Transform** | Plates slide horizontally past each other | Strike-slip faults, earthquakes |

---

### 2. Jamaica's Tectonic Position

Jamaica sits on the **Caribbean Plate**, bordered to the north by the **North American Plate**. The boundary between these two plates runs directly through the Caribbean.

The motion: the Caribbean Plate moves east relative to the North American Plate at approximately **20 millimetres per year**. This is not colliding — it is sliding. The boundary is a **transform fault system**.

The main structure: the **Enriquillo-Plantain Garden Fault Zone (EPGFZ)**.

This fault runs east-west across the Caribbean, passing through Haiti, Jamaica, and continuing west. It is left-lateral — if you stand on one side of the fault and look across it, the opposite side moves to your left.

**In Jamaica**, the EPGFZ passes through Kingston Harbour and beneath the Liguanea Plain — directly under the capital city.

### 3. The 1907 Kingston Earthquake

**January 14, 1907. 3:30 p.m.**

An earthquake estimated at **M 6.5** struck Kingston. The shaking lasted approximately 30 seconds.

The immediate effect: structural collapse of the city's masonry buildings, which were built without seismic design. Thousands of structures failed within seconds.

The secondary effect: **fire**. Gas mains ruptured. Fires spread through the debris. The combination of collapse and fire destroyed roughly 85% of Kingston.

**Death toll: approximately 800 people.**

The Kingston earthquake was not the Caribbean's most deadly — the 2010 Haiti earthquake (M 7.0) on the same fault system killed between 100,000 and 316,000 people.

**Why did the Haiti earthquake kill so many more?**
- Greater magnitude
- Epicentre directly beneath Port-au-Prince (compared to Kingston, where the 1907 epicentre was offshore)
- More densely built informal settlements with no seismic design
- Collapse of governmental buildings including the National Palace

The EPGFZ is capable of producing events up to M 8.0 in some segments. This is not a historical curiosity. It is a present-day engineering constraint on every building in Kingston.

### 4. Seismic Waves — Reading the Earth's Interior

An earthquake is not just destruction. It is information.

**Seismic waves** radiate outward from the earthquake source (the **hypocenter**) and can be detected by seismographs worldwide. The pattern of wave arrivals allows geologists to image the Earth's interior.

**P-waves (Primary / Compressional):**
- Travel through solid AND liquid
- Fastest: ~6 km/s through granite, ~8 km/s in the mantle
- First to arrive at seismographs

**S-waves (Secondary / Shear):**
- Travel through solid only — NOT through liquid
- Slower: ~3.5 km/s through granite
- The observation that S-waves don't reach the far side of the Earth proved the outer core is liquid (iron-nickel, temperature ~4,000°C)

**Surface waves:**
- Travel along the Earth's surface
- Slower than body waves
- Produce the rolling, destructive shaking felt in large earthquakes

The depth to the core was determined not by drilling (we have never drilled below 12 km) but by watching which seismograph stations around the world received S-waves and which didn't.

### 5. The Rock Cycle and Jamaica's Geological History

Jamaica's rocks record approximately 100 million years of history.

**Three rock types:**

**Igneous** — formed from cooled magma/lava:
- Jamaica's oldest rocks are **volcanic**: basalts and tuffs from submarine volcanic activity ~100 Mya
- The Blue Mountains contain **granitic intrusions** — magma that cooled slowly at depth, producing coarse-grained rock
- The Blue Mountain Peak (2,256 m) is underlain by the Blue Mountain Inlier, a block of ancient volcanic and metamorphic rock

**Sedimentary** — formed from compressed sediment:
- The **White Limestone Group** — Jamaica's largest rock unit, covering the central and western parts of the island
- Deposited when Jamaica was submerged beneath a warm tropical sea ~35–60 Mya
- Composed primarily of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) from accumulated shells, coral, and marine organisms
- This is the rock that creates the karst landscape and filters Jamaica's groundwater

**Metamorphic** — formed by heat and pressure:
- Found in the Blue Mountains where tectonic compression recrystallized older rocks
- Schists and phyllites represent ancient ocean floor sediments compressed during tectonic collision

**The rock cycle** describes how rocks transform between these three types over geological time through processes of melting, cooling, erosion, burial, metamorphism, and uplift.

### 6. Bauxite — The Red Soil Economy

**Bauxite** is a sedimentary rock rich in aluminium hydroxide minerals (gibbsite, boehmite, diaspore). It is the world's primary source of aluminium.

Jamaica's bauxite formed through **lateritization**: the intense tropical weathering of limestone and volcanic rock over millions of years leaches away soluble minerals, leaving behind the relatively insoluble aluminium hydroxides as a red-orange surface layer.

Jamaica began exporting bauxite in 1952. For decades, aluminium was Jamaica's largest export earner.

**The processing chain:**
1. Bauxite mined from surface deposits (open-pit)
2. Converted to alumina (Al₂O₃) via the **Bayer Process**: dissolved in hot sodium hydroxide, aluminium hydroxide precipitated, calcined to alumina
3. Alumina smelted to aluminium metal via electrolysis (**Hall-Héroult Process**) — extremely energy-intensive

**The environmental cost:**
- Open-pit mining destroys topsoil and vegetation
- The Bayer Process produces **red mud** — alkaline sodium hydroxide slurry containing heavy metals — stored in tailings ponds
- Sulphate contamination of groundwater (documented in the WRA Atlas: Basin IV Rio Minho sulphate spike to 1,000 mg/L from bauxite operations)
- Cockpit Country: decades of conflict between mining companies and conservationists over whether bauxite beneath the karst forest should be extracted

### 7. Jamaica's Karst — The Cockpit Country

When slightly acidic rainwater (CO₂ dissolved in water forms carbonic acid, H₂CO₃) flows over or through limestone, it dissolves the rock:

```
CaCO₃ + H₂CO₃ → Ca²⁺ + 2HCO₃⁻
```

Over thousands of years, this dissolves caves, sinkholes, and underground channels. The landscape above develops a characteristic topography:
- **Cockpit karst**: conical hills separated by steep-sided, round depressions (the "cockpits")
- The Cockpit Country (Trelawny-St. Elizabeth-St. James) is a global textbook example of this landscape type
- Underground: a labyrinthine system of caves and conduits that carries the island's freshwater
- The Cockpit Country supplies approximately **40% of Jamaica's exploitable freshwater**

Mining in this area would not merely disturb the landscape. It would destroy an aquifer system. Once the conduit network is fractured, the water table does not recover.

### Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Lithosphere | Earth's rigid outer shell, including crust and upper mantle |
| Transform fault | A plate boundary where plates slide horizontally past each other |
| EPGFZ | Enriquillo-Plantain Garden Fault Zone — the main fault boundary through Jamaica |
| P-wave | Primary seismic wave; compressional; travels through all materials |
| S-wave | Secondary seismic wave; shear; cannot travel through liquids |
| Karst | Landscape formed by the dissolution of soluble rocks (limestone) by acidic water |
| Bauxite | Aluminium-rich sedimentary rock; Jamaica's primary mineral export |
| Lateritization | Tropical weathering process that concentrates aluminium and iron in the soil |
| Bayer Process | Industrial method for converting bauxite to alumina (Al₂O₃) |
