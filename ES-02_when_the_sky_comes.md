### Opening Statement

> *"When Jamaicans say 'the sky come down,' they mean more than rain."*

The Caribbean sits in the **North Atlantic Hurricane Belt** — one of the most meteorologically active regions on Earth. Warm ocean water, persistent trade winds, and the geometry of the basin combine to generate storms of extraordinary power.

Jamaica has been struck or directly threatened by major hurricanes in nearly every decade since records began. Hurricane Charlie (1951), Hurricane Gilbert (1988, one of the strongest Atlantic hurricanes ever recorded), Hurricane Ivan (2004), Hurricane Dean (2007), Hurricane Sandy (2012 near-miss), Hurricane Beryl (2024) — the list is not history. It is forecast.

Understanding how these systems form is not academic. In a warming ocean, these systems are becoming more intense. The meteorology of the Caribbean is a matter of survival.

### 1. The Atmosphere — Layers and Pressure

Earth's atmosphere is a thin shell of gas held by gravity. It has mass: the weight of the atmosphere above any point creates **atmospheric pressure** — approximately 101,325 Pa (1 atmosphere) at sea level.

**Atmospheric layers:**

| Layer | Altitude | Key Feature |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Troposphere | 0–12 km | Weather occurs here; temperature decreases with altitude |
| Stratosphere | 12–50 km | Ozone layer; temperature increases with altitude |
| Mesosphere | 50–80 km | Coldest layer; meteors burn here |
| Thermosphere | 80–700 km | Temperature >1,000°C; extremely sparse |
| Exosphere | >700 km | Atmospheric edge; satellites orbit here |

**All weather — every cloud, hurricane, thunderstorm, trade wind — occurs in the troposphere.**

### 2. The Engine of Wind: Pressure Gradients and the Coriolis Effect

Air moves from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure. This pressure gradient creates wind.

But wind does not travel in straight lines.

**The Coriolis Effect:**

The Earth rotates. The surface at the equator moves east at ~1,670 km/h. The surface near the poles barely moves. Air moving from the poles toward the equator is moving "slowly" (in rotational terms) onto a surface that is moving "fast." From the surface's perspective, the air curves westward — to the right in the Northern Hemisphere, to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

For a hurricane in the North Atlantic (north of the equator): air spiraling inward toward the low-pressure centre deflects to the right, producing **counterclockwise rotation**.

This is why all North Atlantic hurricanes spin counterclockwise.

At the equator, the Coriolis effect is zero. This is why tropical storms never form exactly on the equator — there is no rotational forcing to organize the circulation.

### 3. Trade Winds — The Caribbean's Prevailing Winds

The **trade winds** are the dominant surface winds across the tropical Atlantic and Caribbean:

- Blow from the northeast in the Northern Hemisphere (northeast trades)
- Generated by the Hadley Cell circulation: surface air heated at the equator rises, flows poleward at altitude, descends around 30°N (the subtropical highs), and returns to the equator at the surface
- The Coriolis effect deflects this returning flow to the right → northeast winds

The **Caribbean trades** blow from the northeast at 15–25 km/h across Jamaica approximately 8–9 months of the year.

**Effects on Jamaica:**
- Windward (northeast) coasts receive the most rainfall — Portland parish, the Blue Mountain slopes
- Leeward (southwest) coasts are rain-shadowed — the Liguanea Plain, parts of St. Elizabeth
- The trade winds drive the prevailing ocean currents that moderate the island's temperature

The classic Jamaican climate is produced by this northeast trade: warm (25–32°C year-round), moderately humid on the coast, wet and cool in the mountains.

### 4. Hurricane Formation — Six Conditions

A tropical cyclone (hurricane when sustained winds exceed 119 km/h) requires six conditions to form and intensify:

**1. Warm sea surface temperature (SST) ≥ 26.5°C**

The ocean is the fuel. Warm water evaporates intensely, loading the atmosphere with water vapour. As this vapour rises and condenses, it releases **latent heat** — the energy that drives the storm.

This is why hurricanes weaken rapidly over land or cool water.

**2. Sufficient ocean depth (warm water to at least 50m depth)**

A mature hurricane stirs up deeper, cooler water beneath it. If warm water is only in a thin surface layer, the storm cools its own fuel and weakens.

**3. Low atmospheric wind shear**

Wind shear = the change in wind speed or direction with altitude. High shear tilts and tears apart the storm's developing vertical structure.

Atlantic hurricane seasons are partially suppressed in El Niño years because El Niño strengthens upper-level westerly winds (increasing shear) over the Caribbean.

**4. Sufficient atmospheric moisture**

Dry air entraining into the storm core evaporates the cloud water, reducing latent heat release and weakening convection.

**5. Sufficient distance from the equator (at least 5° latitude)**

Coriolis forcing needed for organised rotation.

**6. A pre-existing disturbance**

Tropical cyclones do not form from nothing. Most begin as **African Easterly Waves** — atmospheric waves that propagate westward off the African coast from a region of convective instability along the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ). These waves provide the initial organisation that can, given the right conditions, develop into a depression, then a storm, then a hurricane.

### 5. Hurricane Structure

A mature hurricane is organized into concentric bands around a calm central **eye**:

**Eye (10–60 km diameter):**  
Clear sky. Light winds. Warmest temperatures in the storm (warm core aloft from descending air). Deceptive calm — the most dangerous moment of a landfalling hurricane, because the most destructive winds follow immediately after.

**Eyewall:**  
The most violent part of the storm. The strongest sustained winds, heaviest rainfall, and tallest thunderstorm towers (cumulonimbus reaching 15 km altitude). The primary rain-band and wind damage zone.

**Outer rain bands:**  
Spiral bands of organised convection extending hundreds of kilometres from the centre. Can produce tornadoes, intense rainfall, and flash flooding well outside the eye.

**Storm surge:**  
Wind-driven piling of ocean water against the coastline as the hurricane approaches. A Category 4 storm can produce a surge of 4–6 metres. This is the primary cause of hurricane-related fatalities. The surge inundates coastlines faster than people can evacuate.

### 6. The Saffir-Simpson Scale

| Category | Sustained Winds (km/h) | Damage |
|----------|----------------------|--------|
| Tropical Storm | 63–118 | Branches break, minor flooding |
| Category 1 | 119–153 | Some structural damage |
| Category 2 | 154–177 | Major roof/structural damage |
| Category 3 | 178–208 | Devastating damage; most trees uprooted |
| Category 4 | 209–251 | Catastrophic; most structures destroyed |
| Category 5 | ≥252 | Total destruction; survivable shelter may not exist |

**Hurricane Gilbert (September 12–17, 1988):**
- Made landfall on Jamaica's south coast on September 12
- Peak intensity: Category 5, central pressure 888 mbar — the lowest recorded in Atlantic history at the time
- Sustained winds near 280 km/h
- Jamaica damage: ~45 dead; 500,000 homeless; 80% of banana crop destroyed; most of the island without power for weeks

### 7. Climate Change and Caribbean Hurricanes

The relationship between climate change and hurricane frequency is still debated. The relationship between climate change and **hurricane intensity** is not.

Warmer ocean surface temperatures:
- Provide more fuel → more intense storms
- Expand the geographic range of hurricane-capable waters northward and southward
- Increase **rapid intensification** events: storms that gain ≥55 km/h in 24 hours

Sea level rise:
- Increases storm surge inundation for the same storm intensity
- A 50 cm rise in mean sea level converts a Category 2 storm surge into a Category 3 surge in terms of inundation depth

**Projections for the Caribbean (IPCC AR6, 2021):**
- 1.5°C warming scenario: more intense peak hurricane events, reduced overall frequency
- 2.0°C warming scenario: further intensification; storm surge worsening; Caribbean coral reefs at risk of complete bleaching annually
- Current trajectory (≥2.7°C by 2100): conditions that would produce storms at the upper limit of current Category 5 classification with increasing frequency

The Caribbean contributes approximately **0.1% of global greenhouse gas emissions** and faces a disproportionate share of the consequences.

### 8. CIMH and Caribbean Early Warning Systems

The **Caribbean Institute for Meteorology and Hydrology (CIMH)**, based in Barbados, provides meteorological and hydrological services to the Caribbean:
- Regional climate monitoring and forecasting
- Hurricane tracking and seasonal outlook
- Hydrometeorological services for 16 Caribbean states
- Climate change adaptation research

CIMH is the region's primary scientific institution for the science covered in this lesson. Its work translates meteorological data into the warnings that save lives across the Caribbean basin.

### Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Troposphere | The lowest atmospheric layer (0–12 km), where all weather occurs |
| Coriolis effect | The deflection of moving air due to Earth's rotation (right in NH, left in SH) |
| Trade winds | Persistent northeast surface winds across the tropical Atlantic |
| Latent heat | Energy absorbed or released during phase changes (evaporation/condensation) |
| Wind shear | Change in wind speed or direction with altitude; suppresses hurricane development |
| Storm surge | Wind-driven elevation of ocean water at the coastline |
| Rapid intensification | Increase in sustained winds of ≥55 km/h in 24 hours |
| Saffir-Simpson Scale | Classification of hurricanes by sustained wind speed (Categories 1–5) |
| ITCZ | Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone; equatorial band of rising air and convection |
