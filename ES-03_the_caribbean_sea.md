### Opening Statement

> *"The sea is not background. The sea is the economy, the food supply, the climate regulator, the highway, and the cemetery. To live on an island is to know that the ocean is alive."*

Jamaica's EEZ (Exclusive Economic Zone) covers approximately 258,000 km² — more than 23 times the island's land area. The Caribbean Sea surrounding it is not a passive feature. It is a dynamic, interconnected system of currents, chemistry, and life that has sustained Caribbean people for thousands of years.

It is now changing faster than at any point in the last 10,000 years.

### 1. The Caribbean Sea — Basic Dimensions

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Area | 2.75 million km² |
| Maximum depth | 7,686 m (Cayman Trench — one of the deepest points in the Atlantic basin) |
| Average depth | 2,200 m |
| Average surface temperature | 27–28°C (summer), 24–25°C (winter) |
| Salinity | ~36 ppt (parts per thousand) |
| Major inflows | Amazon and Orinoco river plumes (freshwater input); Atlantic inflow through passages |

The Caribbean Sea is connected to the Atlantic Ocean through multiple channels between the Caribbean islands. The primary inflow is through the **Windward Passage** (between Cuba and Haiti) and the **Mona Passage** (between Puerto Rico and Hispaniola).

### 2. Ocean Currents — The Caribbean as a Highway

**The Caribbean Current:**

Water flows westward through the Caribbean, driven by the Trade Winds pushing surface water in the direction of wind flow. This westward current flows along the south coast of the Greater Antilles, accelerates through the Yucatan Channel, and enters the Gulf of Mexico.

From the Gulf, it exits as the **Florida Current**, which joins the **Gulf Stream** — one of the most powerful ocean currents on Earth. The Gulf Stream transports approximately 30 million cubic metres of water per second (compare: the Amazon discharges ~0.2 million m³/s).

**The global importance:**

The Gulf Stream carries warm tropical water northward along the U.S. Atlantic coast and across the North Atlantic to Western Europe. This is why London (51°N latitude — the same as Calgary, Canada) has mild winters while comparable latitudes in North America are frigid.

The Caribbean is the intake for this system. The warm water that moderates Northern European climate originates in the tropical Caribbean.

**Climate change risk:** The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC), of which the Gulf Stream is a part, is slowing due to freshwater influx from Greenland ice melt. If the AMOC weakens significantly, European climate could cool dramatically even as global average temperatures rise — and Caribbean circulation patterns would be disrupted.

### 3. Ocean Chemistry — What the Sea Is Made Of

**Salinity:**  
Seawater contains approximately 35 grams of dissolved salts per kilogram of water (35 ppt or 3.5%). The major dissolved ions:

| Ion | Symbol | Approximate % of dissolved salts |
|-----|--------|----------------------------------|
| Chloride | Cl⁻ | 55% |
| Sodium | Na⁺ | 30% |
| Sulphate | SO₄²⁻ | 8% |
| Magnesium | Mg²⁺ | 4% |
| Calcium | Ca²⁺ | 1% |
| Potassium | K⁺ | 1% |

**Why does the sea taste salty but rivers don't?**

Rivers carry very small amounts of dissolved minerals — on the order of 0.1 ppt. Over billions of years, rivers have carried these minerals into the ocean, but the ocean doesn't flow out — it evaporates. Evaporation concentrates the salts over geologic time.

**Ocean pH and the carbon cycle:**

The ocean absorbs approximately **25–30% of all CO₂ emitted by human activities**. When CO₂ dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid:

```
CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
```

The H⁺ ions increase acidity. Since pre-industrial times, ocean pH has dropped from approximately 8.2 to 8.1 — a 30% increase in hydrogen ion concentration (because pH is logarithmic).

This **ocean acidification** is dissolving the carbonate shells of marine organisms. At pH 7.95 (projected mid-century under current emissions), aragonite — the form of calcium carbonate coral skeletons are made from — becomes undersaturated in Caribbean waters. Corals dissolve faster than they can grow.

### 4. Coral Reefs — The Ocean's Tropical Forests

Coral reefs cover approximately 0.1% of the ocean floor but support an estimated 25% of all marine species.

Jamaica's reefs once ranked among the most diverse and productive in the Western Atlantic. They no longer do.

**What a coral is:**

A coral is not a plant. It is an animal — specifically, a colony of tiny polyps (Cnidaria phylum). Each polyp is a centimetre-scale tube of tissue with a mouth surrounded by stinging tentacles, secreting a calcium carbonate skeleton.

The coral polyp harbours **zooxanthellae** — photosynthetic algae (dinoflagellates of the genus Symbiodinium) living within its tissue. This is a mutualism:
- The zooxanthellae photosynthesise, providing the polyp with up to 90% of its energy
- The polyp provides the zooxanthellae with a protected location and the CO₂ they need

The zooxanthellae give coral its colour — the yellow-green-brown tones visible underwater. They also give coral reefs their extraordinary productivity: the symbiosis allows corals to build massive calcium carbonate structures even in nutrient-poor tropical waters.

**Coral bleaching:**

When water temperatures rise above the coral's thermal tolerance (~1°C above the summer maximum for more than a week), the polyp expels its zooxanthellae. Without the algae, the white calcium carbonate skeleton shows through the transparent tissue: **bleaching**.

A bleached coral is not immediately dead — it is stressed. If temperatures return to normal within weeks, the zooxanthellae can repopulate and the coral recovers. If the thermal stress persists, the coral starves and dies.

The dead skeleton is rapidly colonised by algae, turning the reef a grey-green colour. Recovery to a complex reef ecosystem, if it occurs at all, takes decades.

### 5. Jamaica's Reef Collapse

In the 1970s, Jamaica's reefs had approximately **50–70% coral cover** — dense, three-dimensional structures hosting extraordinary biodiversity.

By the late 1990s, coral cover had fallen to below **10%** across most Jamaican reefs.

**The causes:**

**1. Overfishing (1960s–present):**
- Removal of parrotfish and surgeonfish — the primary grazers that keep algae cropped and allow coral larvae to settle on bare substrate
- Without grazing fish, algae outcompetes coral for space

**2. The 1983 Diadema die-off:**
- A mass mortality event killed ~97% of *Diadema antillarum* (long-spined sea urchin) across the Caribbean
- Diadema were the primary algal grazers after overfishing had removed the fish
- With Diadema gone, algae exploded, smothering recovering coral

**3. Agricultural runoff:**
- Fertiliser nitrates promote algal growth
- Increased turbidity from eroded soil reduces light penetration, suppressing zooxanthellae photosynthesis

**4. 1980 and 1988 hurricanes:**
- Hurricane Allen (1980) and Hurricane Gilbert (1988) caused direct physical damage to reef structures

**5. Bleaching events:**
- 1987–88, 1998, 2005, 2010, 2016–17, 2023–24 — each event kills corals weakened by previous stressors

The 1998 mass bleaching event (triggered by the El Niño-enhanced thermal anomaly) killed approximately **50% of Caribbean corals** in a single year.

### 6. The Sea Level Equation

Global mean sea level is rising due to two factors:

**Thermal expansion:** Water expands as it warms. The oceans have absorbed ~90% of the excess heat trapped by greenhouse gases. Thermal expansion currently contributes approximately 40% of observed sea level rise.

**Ice melt:** The Greenland Ice Sheet and West Antarctic Ice Sheet are losing mass. Meltwater flows to the ocean. The remainder of sea level rise comes from ice melt.

**Current rate:** Global mean sea level is rising at approximately 3.7 mm/year (2006–2018 average, IPCC AR6).

**For Jamaica specifically:**

Jamaica does not just experience global mean sea level rise. Local sea level is modulated by:
- Regional ocean circulation patterns (the Caribbean sits in a zone of slightly accelerated rise relative to global mean)
- Vertical land movement (some parts of Jamaica are slowly subsiding)
- Storm surge interacts multiplicatively with higher baseline sea level

The combination of sea level rise, more intense storm surges, and coral reef degradation (which reduces the natural wave-breaking function of reefs) creates compounding risk for Jamaica's coastal communities.

### 7. Marine Food Web

The Caribbean marine food web is anchored by:

**Primary producers:**
- **Phytoplankton** — microscopic algae and cyanobacteria; the base of the open ocean food web
- **Seagrasses** — underwater meadows (Thalassia testudinum, turtle grass) covering bay floors; critical habitat for juvenile fish, turtles, manatees
- **Coral reef algae** — supported by reef structure and light penetration

**Primary consumers:**
- Zooplankton (copepods, krill larvae)
- Parrotfish, surgeonfish (coralline algae grazers)
- Sea urchins (benthic algae)
- Sea turtles (seagrasses)

**Secondary consumers:**
- Jacks, snappers, groupers — the commercial fish species of Jamaica
- Octopus, squid
- Dolphins

**Apex predators:**
- Caribbean reef sharks (*Carcharhinus perezi*)
- Hammerheads, tiger sharks in deeper water

The removal of apex predators (sharks) and large predatory fish (groupers) through overfishing triggers **trophic cascades** — destabilising the entire food web from the top down.
